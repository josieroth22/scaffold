# State-Specific College Financial Aid Programs
## Reference Document for Scaffold

*Last updated: February 2026*
*Sources: Official state agency websites, verified via web search*

---

## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

This reference is organized into five sections:

1. **Merit-Based Scholarships** -- Programs with specific GPA and test score thresholds. These are the best "financial floor" candidates because families can plan around hitting concrete numbers years in advance.
2. **Automatic Admission Rules** -- State laws guaranteeing admission based on class rank (Texas, California).
3. **State 4-Year Financial Aid Programs** -- Need-based, merit-based, and hybrid programs covering tuition at 4-year institutions. Organized alphabetically within the section.
4. **Community College Promise Programs** -- State programs covering 2-year tuition. Listed alphabetically.
5. **Regional & Unique Programs** -- DC TAG, WUE, MSEP, and other cross-state programs.

Each program entry includes eligibility requirements, what it covers, and a **Scaffold note** explaining how the AI prompt should use the information when generating a family's plan.

At the bottom, **Notes for the Scaffold Prompt** provides 17 rules for how the AI should handle state aid across all plans.

## 1. MERIT-BASED SCHOLARSHIPS

These programs have clear, published academic thresholds. Families can plan around hitting these numbers years in advance, making them ideal for Scaffold's developmental roadmap.

---

### GEORGIA -- HOPE Scholarship & Zell Miller Scholarship

**HOPE Scholarship**
- Requirements: 3.0 GPA (as calculated by Georgia Student Finance Commission), Georgia resident, graduate from eligible GA high school
- Covers: Partial tuition at Georgia public colleges and universities
- Renewal: 3.0 cumulative GPA in college, checked at 30/60/90 attempted hours and end of each spring semester
- Duration: Up to 127 semester hours or first bachelor's degree

**Zell Miller Scholarship**
- Requirements: 3.7 GPA + 1200 SAT (math + reading) OR 25 ACT composite, from a single sitting. Also requires four rigor courses (AP, IB, or dual enrollment). Valedictorians and salutatorians automatically qualify.
- Covers: 100% of tuition at Georgia public colleges and universities. At Georgia Tech, that's roughly $10,500/year for two semesters at 15 credit hours.
- Renewal: 3.3 cumulative GPA in college
- Duration: Up to 127 semester hours, 10-year eligibility window
- Key detail: If you lose Zell Miller but maintain 3.0, you drop to HOPE. Can regain Zell Miller one time at a checkpoint.
- STEM boost: 0.5 GPA points added for B/C/D grades in approved STEM courses

**Scaffold note:** Zell Miller is one of the strongest "financial floor" programs in the country. For any Georgia family, the plan should explicitly identify "protect the 3.7 GPA and get to 1200 SAT" as the single most important financial move.

### FLORIDA -- Bright Futures Scholarship Program

**Florida Academic Scholars (FAS)**
- Requirements: 3.5 weighted GPA in 16 college-prep core courses, 1330 SAT or 29 ACT (Class of 2026+), 100 hours of community service or paid work (75 service hours for students entering 9th grade in 2024-25 and beyond)
- Covers: 100% of tuition and applicable fees at Florida public institutions, plus $300/semester for additional costs. Comparable award at private Florida schools.
- Note: Test score thresholds change by graduating class.

**Florida Medallion Scholars (FMS)**
- Requirements: 3.0 weighted GPA in core courses, 1190 SAT or 24 ACT (Class of 2026+), 75 service hours or 100 paid work hours or 100 combined
- Covers: 75% of tuition and applicable fees at Florida public institutions

**Florida Gold Seal CAPE Scholars**
- Requirements: 3.0 weighted core GPA + 3.5 GPA in 3 years of the same vocational program
- Covers: Fixed per-credit-hour amount for career and technical programs

**General Bright Futures rules:**
- Florida resident and US citizen or eligible noncitizen
- Must graduate from a Florida high school
- Apply via the Florida Financial Aid Application (FFAA) by August 31 after graduation
- Available at Florida public and qualifying private institutions only
- Minimum 6 credits per semester to receive funding
- Renewal requirements vary by tier

**Scaffold note:** FAS is Florida's equivalent of Georgia's Zell Miller. The plan should build the course selection strategy around hitting the GPA and test score thresholds. The community service/work hours requirement is easy to miss and should be flagged early, starting freshman year of high school.

### LOUISIANA -- TOPS (Taylor Opportunity Program for Students)

Five award levels, from least to most competitive:

**TOPS Tech:** 2.5 core GPA + ACT 17 (or Silver level WorkKeys) + 21 TOPS Tech Jumpstart Core Curriculum units. Covers tuition at 2-year Louisiana public institutions.

**TOPS Opportunity:** 2.5 core GPA + ACT 20 + 19 TOPS Core Curriculum units. Covers tuition at Louisiana public institutions.

**TOPS Performance:** 3.25 core GPA (Class of 2022+) + ACT 23. Covers tuition + $400/year stipend.

**TOPS Honors:** 3.5 core GPA + ACT 27. Covers tuition + $800/year stipend.

**TOPS Excellence (NEW, added 2025):** 3.5 core GPA + ACT 31. Covers full tuition and fees or $12,000/year, whichever is less. At private LAICU institutions: tuition and fees or $8,500/year.

**General TOPS rules:**
- Louisiana resident (parents too) for at least 2 years prior to July 1 of enrollment year
- Must attend a BESE-approved high school (all LA public schools qualify; private schools must be approved)
- Must enroll full-time by the first semester following the first anniversary of high school graduation
- TOPS does NOT superscore ACT/SAT. Highest composite from a single sitting is used.
- The Opportunity tier ACT minimum is the prior year's state average, but never less than 20
- Renewal: Full-time enrollment, earn 24 credits/year, maintain 2.0 cumulative GPA. Performance, Honors, and Excellence require 3.0 cumulative, checked at end of each spring.
- Now accepts the Classical Learning Test (CLT) in addition to ACT and SAT

**Scaffold note:** TOPS has a lower floor than most state merit programs (ACT 17 for Tech, ACT 20 for Opportunity), making it accessible to a wide range of students. The tiered structure means the plan can show families exactly what each ACT score improvement is worth in dollars. The new Excellence tier is a big deal for high-achieving Louisiana students.

### SOUTH CAROLINA -- Palmetto Fellows, LIFE, and HOPE

**Palmetto Fellows Scholarship**
- Standard path: Top 6% class rank + 1200 SAT or 25 ACT + 3.5 cumulative GPA on SC Uniform Grading Policy
- Alternate path (no rank required): 1400 SAT or 31 ACT + 4.0 cumulative GPA
- Award: $6,700 freshman year, $7,500 sophomore through senior years
- STEM Enhancement: Additional $2,500/year for STEM majors starting sophomore year (must complete 14 credits of math/science by end of first year)
- Renewal: 3.0 cumulative GPA, 30 credit hours per year
- Must attend an eligible SC four-year institution
- SAT superscoring allowed. ACT composite from multiple sittings allowed.

**SC LIFE Scholarship**
- Requirements: Two of the following three: 3.0 GPA on SC Uniform Grading Policy, 1100 SAT or 22 ACT (effective Fall 2023+), top 30% of class
- Award: Up to $5,000/year ($4,700 tuition + $300 books) at four-year institutions
- Cannot receive both Palmetto Fellows and LIFE in the same year

**SC HOPE Scholarship**
- Requirements: 3.0 GPA (for students who don't qualify for LIFE)
- Award: Up to $2,800 freshman year only. Non-renewable.

**Scaffold note:** South Carolina has a three-tier system. The plan should identify which tier the student is most likely to hit and what it would take to reach the next one. The STEM enhancement for Palmetto Fellows ($2,500/year extra) is worth noting for any STEM-inclined student.

### TENNESSEE -- HOPE Scholarship & Related Programs

**Tennessee HOPE Scholarship**
- Requirements: 3.0 GPA or 21 ACT / 1060 SAT, Tennessee resident
- Award at 4-year institutions: $2,250/semester ($4,500/year) for freshmen and sophomores, $2,850/semester ($5,700/year) for juniors and seniors
- Award at 2-year institutions: $1,500/semester ($3,000/year)
- Renewal: Continuous enrollment, 2.75 cumulative GPA at 24 and 48 attempted hours, 3.0 GPA at 72/96/120 hours

**Tennessee HOPE Access Grant**
- Requirements: 2.75 GPA, 18-20 ACT, household AGI $36,000 or less
- Award: $2,650/year at 4-year institutions, $1,700/year at 2-year institutions (freshman and sophomore years only)

**Aspire Award (supplement to HOPE)**
- For HOPE-eligible students with household AGI of $36,000 or less
- Additional $500/semester ($1,000/year) at 4-year institutions, $250/semester at 2-year institutions

**General Assist Award**
- Tennessee resident with financial need, enrolled at least half-time
- Up to $2,000/year

**Scaffold note:** Tennessee's HOPE is less generous than Georgia's but still significant. The plan should pair it with Tennessee Promise (see community college section) for families considering a 2-year start.

### KENTUCKY -- Kentucky Educational Excellence Scholarship (KEES)

KEES is unusual: it accumulates year by year through high school based on each year's GPA, with a bonus added for ACT scores.

- GPA awards: 2.5 GPA earns $125/year, scaling up to $500/year for 4.0. Each year of high school is calculated separately.
- ACT bonus: 15 ACT earns $36, scaling up to $500 for 28+
- AP/IB supplemental awards available for low-income students (free/reduced lunch eligible)
- Maximum possible award: roughly $2,500/year (combining all four years of GPA awards + ACT bonus)
- Covers: Annual award at any Kentucky college or university
- Renewal: 3.0+ GPA gets full award. 2.5-2.99 may receive reduced award depending on "on track to graduate" status. Below 2.5 loses the award for the following year.
- Duration: 8 semesters or 5 years from high school graduation, whichever comes first

**Scaffold note:** Because KEES accumulates year by year, the plan should show families the cumulative value of maintaining high grades each year, not just senior year. A student who earns a 4.0 every year of high school and scores 28+ on the ACT locks in $2,500/year for college without filing a single application.

### WYOMING -- Hathaway Scholarship

Wyoming's only state scholarship program. Four merit-based tiers for graduates attending Wyoming community colleges or the University of Wyoming:

| Tier | GPA | ACT | Per Semester |
|------|-----|-----|-------------|
| Honors | 3.5 | 25 | $1,680 |
| Performance | 3.0 | 21 | $1,260 |
| Opportunity | 2.5 | 19 | $840 |
| Provisional | 2.5 | 17 | $840 (community college only) |

- Must complete the Hathaway Success Curriculum (4 years each of math, English, science; 3 years social studies; 4 years of foreign language, arts, or CTE)
- ACT superscoring is allowed
- Renewal: 2.5 GPA for Honors/Performance/Opportunity (if GPA drops below 2.5 but stays above 2.25, student drops to Opportunity level). 2.25 GPA for Provisional.
- Duration: 8 full-time semesters (96 credits) over 6 years
- Need-based supplement available if unmet need exceeds $2,000 (requires FAFSA)
- Must initiate within 4 years of high school graduation
- Provisional students can transfer from community college to UW after earning an associate degree

**Scaffold note:** Because Hathaway is Wyoming's entire state financial aid conversation, the plan should spell out exactly what each GPA/ACT improvement is worth in dollars. For Provisional students, the community college path to UW is a built-in feature of the program, not a workaround.

### WEST VIRGINIA -- PROMISE Scholarship

- Requirements: 3.0 core GPA + 22 ACT composite (with sub-scores of 20+ in each section) or equivalent SAT
- Covers: Full tuition and mandatory fees at WV public institutions, comparable amount at private WV institutions
- Renewal: 2.75 GPA + 30 credits/year in the first year, 3.0 GPA thereafter

**Scaffold note:** PROMISE is West Virginia's flagship merit scholarship and covers full tuition, making it the most important financial target for WV families. The ACT sub-score requirement (20+ in each section) is easy to overlook. A student with a 24 composite but a 19 in one section would not qualify. The plan should flag both the composite and sub-score targets.

---

### MISSISSIPPI -- Eminent Scholars Grant, MTAG, and MESG

**Eminent Scholars Grant:** 3.5 GPA + 29 ACT. Covers full tuition at Mississippi public institutions.

**MTAG (Tuition Assistance Grant):** 2.5 GPA + 15 ACT (public schools) or 2.5 GPA (private schools). Award varies by institution type.

**MESG (Education Scholars Grant):** 3.5 GPA + 29 ACT. Covers up to full tuition equivalent at private Mississippi institutions.

**Scaffold note:** Mississippi has a two-track system: the Eminent Scholars Grant and MESG target high achievers (3.5 GPA + 29 ACT) at public and private schools respectively, while MTAG provides a lower-threshold option (2.5 GPA + 15 ACT). For families with strong students, the 29 ACT target is worth building toward. For others, MTAG ensures some state support is available at a much lower bar.

---

### ALASKA -- Performance Scholarship

Three tiers. Level I: 3.5 GPA + 25 ACT. Level II: 3.0 GPA + 23 ACT. Level III: 2.5 GPA + 20 ACT. Awards up to $4,755/year (Level I) at Alaska institutions. Must complete specific core curriculum.

**Scaffold note:** Alaska's tiered structure means the plan should show families exactly what each GPA/ACT improvement unlocks. The required core curriculum must be planned early in high school. Because Alaska has limited in-state college options, the plan should also flag WUE eligibility for students considering schools in other western states.

## 2. AUTOMATIC ADMISSION RULES

---

### TEXAS -- Top % Rule

**At all Texas public universities:** Top 10% of graduating class = guaranteed admission.

**At UT Austin (special rules):**
- Fall 2025 / spring 2026 applicants: Top 6%
- Fall 2026 and beyond: Top 5%
- By law, 75% of UT Austin's in-state freshman seats must go to auto-admitted students
- Important: Auto-admit guarantees a SEAT, not a specific MAJOR. Competitive programs (engineering, business, CS, nursing) run their own selection.
- For Texas A&M: Top 10% still applies

**Scaffold note:** For Texas families, class rank is everything. The plan should explain the competitive dynamics of the student's specific high school (rank is relative to peers, so a 4.0 at one school might be top 3% and at another might be top 15%). The plan should also be clear that auto-admit to UT doesn't mean auto-admit to McCombs or Cockrell.

### CALIFORNIA -- UC Eligibility in the Local Context (ELC)

- Top 9% of each California high school's graduating class is identified and guaranteed admission to at least one UC campus (not necessarily their first choice)
- Based on GPA in UC-required coursework (a-g courses)
- Separate from general UC admission, which uses holistic review

## 3. STATE 4-YEAR FINANCIAL AID PROGRAMS

---

### NEW YORK -- Excelsior Scholarship

- Income limit: $125,000 household AGI or less
- Covers: Tuition-free at SUNY or CUNY (2-year and 4-year), last-dollar after Pell and TAP
- Requirements: 30 credits/year, continuous full-time enrollment
- Post-graduation obligation: Must live and work in NY for a number of years equal to years of scholarship received. If you leave, it converts to a no-interest loan.
- Timeline: 5 years to complete bachelor's, 3 for associate's

**Scaffold note:** The residency/work requirement is a real constraint. The plan should flag this explicitly for any NY family considering Excelsior. A student who receives Excelsior for four years owes four years of living and working in New York.

---

### NEW MEXICO -- Opportunity Scholarship

- No income cap. No separate application required (automatically awarded by the institution).
- Covers: Tuition and fees at all 29 NM public colleges and four-year universities
- Requirements: NM resident 12+ months, 2.5 GPA in college, at least 6 credits/semester (12 for recent high school graduates in their first term)
- Recent graduates must enroll within 16 months of graduation, complete 30 credits/year at 4-year institutions or 24 credits/year at 2-year institutions
- Returning learners must enroll in 6+ credits
- Duration: Up to 160 attempted credit hours for bachelor's, 90 for associate's

**Scaffold note:** For NM families, this is one of the best financial floors in the country. No income limit, no application, covers four-year schools. The plan should model what attending UNM or NMSU looks like with Opportunity covering tuition, layered with Pell for other costs.

---

### INDIANA -- 21st Century Scholars

- Must enroll in 7th or 8th grade. Auto-enrolled for income-eligible students in the classes of 2028 and beyond.
- Initial income threshold: Based on free/reduced lunch eligibility, roughly 185% of Federal Poverty Level (about $59,000 for a family of four; adjusts annually)
- College-entry financial means test: 400% of FPL (roughly $127,000 for a family of four for the Class of 2025; varies by family size and year)
- Scholar Pledge: Graduate with Core 40 diploma, 2.5+ GPA, complete Scholar Success Program activities, no felonies or drug use
- Covers: Up to 100% of tuition at Indiana public colleges and universities, comparable award at private Indiana schools
- Duration: Up to 4 years
- Must complete 30 credits/year in college

**Scaffold note:** This is a critical "early action" program. For Indiana families with kids in 6th or 7th grade, the plan MUST flag this. Missing the 8th grade enrollment deadline means missing the scholarship entirely. With automatic enrollment now in effect for recent classes, the plan should tell families to check ScholarTrack.IN.gov to confirm enrollment.

### OKLAHOMA -- Oklahoma's Promise

- Apply: 8th through 12th grade (deadline December 31 of senior year, but earlier is better)
- Income limit at application: $60,000 AGI (1-2 dependent children), $70,000 (3-4 children), $80,000 (5+ children)
- Income limit in college: $100,000 AGI, checked annually via FAFSA
- High school requirements: Complete 15-unit college-prep core curriculum (or 17 units for full college admission eligibility, which adds foreign language and fine arts), 2.5 cumulative GPA in those courses AND overall, no felonies or drug abuse
- Covers: Full tuition at any Oklahoma public college or university, or partial tuition at accredited Oklahoma private colleges and career technology centers
- Duration: Up to 5 years or 129 credit hours or bachelor's degree, whichever comes first
- Special provision: Children of Oklahoma public school teachers with 10+ years of service have expanded income eligibility (700% of Federal Poverty Level)

**Scaffold note:** Oklahoma's Promise is one of the best "early commitment" programs in the country. For Oklahoma families with kids in middle school, this should be flagged immediately. The income check happens TWICE: once at application (lower thresholds) and again each year in college ($100K cap). Families whose income might rise should apply early while they still qualify.

### WASHINGTON -- Washington College Grant

One of the most generous need-based programs in the country. Replaced the old State Need Grant in 2019.

- Income thresholds (family of four, 2025-26):
  - Full award (covers 100% of tuition): Household income at or below roughly $78,500 (60% of state median family income)
  - Partial awards: Available up to roughly $131,000 (100% of state MFI)
  - Awards decrease on a sliding scale as income rises
- Maximum award: Up to roughly $12,780/year at UW (varies by institution type)
- Works at all Washington public 2-year and 4-year institutions, many private institutions, and state-registered apprenticeship programs
- No GPA or test score requirement
- Must file FAFSA or WASFA (for undocumented students)
- Guaranteed funding for all eligible students (no waitlist since 2020)

**Scaffold note:** WA Grant can cover full tuition at UW for families under roughly $78K and provides meaningful partial awards well into the middle class ($131K). Unlike many state programs, there's no merit component at all. The plan should model cost of attendance at UW or WSU with WA Grant applied.

### TEXAS -- TEXAS Grant (Toward EXcellence, Access, and Success)

- Requirements: Texas resident, demonstrated financial need (FAFSA required), enrolled at a Texas public university, must not have attempted more than 30 semester credit hours at time of initial award (excluding dual enrollment and credit-by-exam hours), no felony drug convictions
- Award: Up to $10,858/year at universities ($5,429/semester maximum for 2025-26; varies by institution based on cost of attendance and available funding). Target award is $5,000/year ($2,500/semester).
- Renewal: Must complete at least 24 semester credit hours per year, maintain 75% course completion rate and 2.5 cumulative GPA (checked at end of second year and each subsequent year). First-year students must meet institutional satisfactory academic progress standards.
- Duration: Up to 150 attempted semester credit hours or receipt of a bachelor's degree, whichever comes first
- Key details: Available only at Texas public universities (not community colleges, which have TEOG instead). Students must enroll within 16 months of high school graduation. Priority consideration given to students with SAI at or below roughly $6,500. The grant is need-based with no merit component.
- Note: For Texas automatic admission rules (Top 10% / Top 6% at UT Austin), see Section 2.

**Scaffold note:** For Texas families with financial need, the TEXAS Grant combined with the Top % auto-admit rule (Section 2) creates one of the most powerful state-level combos in the country. A student who ranks in the top 6-10% of their class gets guaranteed admission to a Texas public university, and if the family qualifies financially, the TEXAS Grant can cover a large share of tuition on top of that. The plan should model UT Austin or Texas A&M with TEXAS Grant + Pell layered together. Flag the 16-month enrollment window and the 30-credit-hour cap for initial eligibility, since dual enrollment credits (excluded from the cap) will not disqualify the student.

### CALIFORNIA -- Cal Grant

- Cal Grant A: Need-based + 3.0 GPA minimum. Covers tuition at UC (up to roughly $14,000/year), CSU (roughly $5,742/year), or private schools (up to $9,220/year).
- Cal Grant B: Need-based + 2.0 GPA minimum. First year covers living allowance only (roughly $1,648); subsequent years add tuition like Cal Grant A.
- Cal Grant C: For vocational and technical students. Covers books, supplies, and tuition at private schools.
- Competitive Cal Grants: Roughly 11,000 awarded annually to students who don't qualify for entitlement grants.
- Deadline: March 2 each year (FAFSA + GPA verification)

**California Middle Class Scholarship:** For families with income up to $234,000 (2025-26; adjusts annually). Award varies by student based on cost of attendance minus other aid. Available at UC, CSU, and select community college bachelor's programs.

### ARKANSAS -- Academic Challenge Scholarship & ArFuture Grant

**Academic Challenge Scholarship**
- Requirements: ACT 19 (or equivalent), Arkansas resident 12+ months, pursuing first bachelor's
- Award at 4-year institutions: $1,000 freshman year, $4,000 sophomore and junior years, $5,000 senior year
- Award at 2-year institutions: $1,000 first year, $3,000 second year
- Additional "PLUS" funding: Up to $2,500/semester for students with SAI below 8,000
- Renewal: 2.5 GPA, earn 27 credits first year then 30 credits/year after
- Apply by July 1 of senior year via SAMS (sams.adhe.edu)
- Funded by the Arkansas Scholarship Lottery

**ArFuture Grant**
- Covers tuition and fees for certificate and associate degree programs at Arkansas public institutions
- Must enroll in STEM or designated high-demand program
- No GPA or test score requirement beyond high school graduation

**Governor's Distinguished Scholarship:** Up to $5,000/year for top academic performers. Can be combined with Academic Challenge (total cannot exceed $10,000/year).

**Scaffold note:** Arkansas has a layered system. The Academic Challenge is the workhorse program (ACT 19 is a relatively low bar), and ArFuture fills the gap for community college and career-tech paths. The escalating award structure ($1K to $5K) rewards persistence.

### ILLINOIS -- Monetary Award Program (MAP Grant)

- Need-based. No GPA or test score requirement.
- Maximum award: Up to $8,064/year (2025-26; statutory max is $8,400 before a 4% reduction factor)
- Must have SAI (Student Aid Index) of 9,000 or less
- Must be Illinois resident, enrolled at least 3 credit hours (awards are prorated; full award at 15+ credits)
- Must file FAFSA by the suspension date (typically early spring; funds run out, so apply EARLY)
- Available at approved Illinois public and private 2-year and 4-year colleges
- Maximum of 135 MAP Paid Credit Hours total
- Funded by Illinois General Assembly (roughly $722 million for 2025-26)
- Undocumented students can apply via the Alternative Application for Illinois Financial Aid (under the RISE Act)

**Scaffold note:** MAP is purely need-based and substantial ($8K/year), but funding is first-come-first-served and routinely runs out. The plan should emphasize filing the FAFSA as early as possible. For Illinois families, combining MAP with Pell can make community college or a state university very affordable.

---

### IDAHO -- Opportunity Scholarship & LAUNCH

**Idaho Opportunity Scholarship**
- Requirements: 2.7 unweighted GPA, Idaho resident, graduated from Idaho high school or equivalent
- Award: Up to $3,500/year, renewable for up to 4 years ($14,000 total)
- Scoring: 70% based on SAI (from FAFSA), 30% based on GPA
- FAFSA deadline: March 1
- Renewal: 2.7 GPA in college, 30 credits/year, satisfactory academic progress
- Available at eligible Idaho colleges and universities (note: community colleges are no longer eligible for new recipients as of 2023-24)

**Idaho LAUNCH**
- One-time grant covering up to 80% of tuition and fees, max $8,000
- For Idaho high school graduates pursuing in-demand career programs
- Roughly $75 million in funding, 9,000-10,000 awards per year
- Priority application deadline: November 30
- Cannot receive both LAUNCH and Opportunity Scholarship simultaneously

**Scaffold note:** Idaho families need to choose between Opportunity (renewable, smaller annual amount) and LAUNCH (one-time, larger amount). For students headed to 4-year universities, Opportunity is usually better over time. For career-tech certificates, LAUNCH may be the better fit.

### MICHIGAN -- Reconnect & Achievement Scholarship

**Michigan Reconnect**
- Free in-district tuition at Michigan community colleges for adults 25+
- Michigan resident 1+ year, high school diploma or GED, no prior college degree
- Last-dollar scholarship (Pell applied first). Must earn 12 credits/year and maintain 2.0 GPA.

**Michigan Achievement Scholarship / Community College Guarantee**
- Tuition-free community college for recent high school graduates

**Scaffold note:** Reconnect is primarily for adults returning to school, so it's less relevant for Scaffold's typical K-12 audience. The Michigan Achievement Scholarship for recent graduates IS relevant and should be flagged for Michigan families considering a community college start.

---

### NORTH CAROLINA -- Next NC Scholarship & NC Promise

**Next NC Scholarship (replaces NC Education Lottery Scholarship, starting 2024-25)**
- Combines Pell Grant with state-funded financial aid into one package
- At UNC System universities: At least $5,000/year for eligible students
- At NC community colleges: At least $3,000/year
- Eligibility: NC resident, financial need (approximately AGI under $80,000, tied to SAI)
- Can cover tuition, fees, books, food, and housing
- Must file FAFSA (priority deadline: June 1 for UNC System, August 15 for community colleges)

**NC Promise Tuition Plan**
- Reduces tuition to $500/semester for ALL undergraduates (no eligibility requirements) at four UNC System schools:
  - Elizabeth City State University

### MINNESOTA -- North Star Promise Scholarship

### DC TAG (DC Tuition Assistance Grant)

- DC resident (domiciled) for 12+ months, pursuing first bachelor's or associate's degree
- At public universities nationwide: Up to $15,000/year toward the difference between in-state and out-of-state tuition (lifetime max $75,000)
- At private HBCUs nationwide and private nonprofits in DC metro area: Up to $3,750/year (lifetime max $18,750)
- Duration: Up to 6 years from first semester enrolled
- No GPA or test score requirement for initial eligibility
- Must be enrolled at least half-time and maintain satisfactory academic progress
- Note: Income limit is statutory max of $1M but effective threshold for recent cohorts is approximately $624K (adjusted for inflation by entry year)

**Scaffold note:** DC TAG is one of the most unique programs in the country. Because DC has no flagship state university, the program gives DC residents a tuition discount at hundreds of public universities nationwide. For any DC family, the plan should model cost of attendance at target public schools with $15K/year DC TAG applied. This can make out-of-state publics like UVA, Maryland, or Michigan meaningfully more affordable.

### Western Undergraduate Exchange (WUE)

- Administered by the Western Interstate Commission for Higher Education (WICHE)
- Students from participating western states pay 150% of in-state tuition (instead of full out-of-state rates) at participating institutions in other western states
- Participating states: AK, AZ, CA, CO, HI, ID, MT, NV, NM, ND, OR, SD, UT, WA, WY, and Pacific territories (American Samoa, CNMI, Guam)
- Over 160 participating institutions
- Savings can be $10,000-$20,000/year compared to full out-of-state tuition
- Some schools and programs are excluded; WUE is competitive at many institutions

**Scaffold note:** WUE can dramatically expand the list of affordable options for families in western states. The plan should flag WUE-eligible schools that are good fits and model the WUE rate vs. in-state at the family's home state flagship.

### ALABAMA -- Alabama Student Assistance Program (ASAP)

**Alabama Student Assistance Program (ASAP)**

The real financial leverage comes from institutional merit (e.g., UA's automatic merit ladder, Auburn's competitive awards).

**CCSNH Promise Program**
- Requirements: NH resident, Pell-eligible, enrolled in at least 6 credits at one of NH's seven community colleges. Must complete FAFSA.
- Covers: Last-dollar coverage of remaining tuition and fees after all other grants/scholarships. Effectively makes community college tuition-free for Pell-eligible students.
- Key details: No separate application required  automatically considered when FAFSA is filed. Launched 2023-24.

**Granite Guarantee (University System of NH)**
- Requirements: NH resident, Pell-eligible, first-time student or CCSNH graduate. Must enroll full-time at UNH, Plymouth State, Keene State, or Granite State College.
- Covers: Full tuition waiver for fall/spring semesters. Does NOT cover fees, room, board, or other expenses.
- Duration: Up to 4 years (8 semesters) for first-time students; up to 2 years for CCSNH transfers.
- Key details: At UNH, mandatory fees can exceed tuition, so "tuition-free" does not mean "free." Room and board are additional.

**Scaffold note:** For Pell-eligible NH families, the Promise + Granite Guarantee combination creates a powerful 2+2 pathway: free community college via Promise, then free tuition at a state university via Granite Guarantee. But critically flag that Granite Guarantee covers tuition only  not fees. At UNH, remaining costs can still be significant. NH offers limited state aid for non-Pell families.

### NORTH DAKOTA -- North Dakota Scholarship

**North Dakota Scholarship (Class of 2025+, replaces ND Academic and CTE Scholarships)**
- Requirements: Must fulfill all "Essential Skills" requirements AND meet at least 2 of 3 pathway components: Post-Secondary Ready (ACT 24+), Workforce Ready (4 CTE credits + ACT 24 or WorkKeys 5s + 2 additional indicators), or Military Ready. Must maintain 2.75 cumulative GPA in college.
- Covers: $750/semester ($1,500/year). Total maximum $6,000.
- Renewal: GPA monitored each term. One warning for GPA below 2.75; second failure permanently suspends eligibility.
- Duration: 6 years following high school graduation to use full $6,000.
- Key details: Modest award. The Essential Skills + pathway structure is more complex than a simple GPA/test cutoff, so students must plan high school curriculum carefully. Students who earned the old Academic/CTE Scholarship before 2025 continue under prior rules.

**Scaffold note:** For North Dakota families, note the ND Scholarship as helpful but modest ($1,500/year). Flag the curriculum planning needed to meet Essential Skills + pathway requirements. ND public university tuition is already among the lowest in the nation, so even without large state scholarships, in-state options are affordable.

### OHIO -- Ohio College Opportunity Grant (OCOG), Buckeye Bridge Initiative

**Ohio College Opportunity Grant (OCOG)**
- Requirements: Ohio resident, SAI of 3,750 or less, household income $96,000 or less, enrolled in associate or bachelor's program at eligible Ohio institution (public or private nonprofit). Must complete FAFSA by October 1 (unusually early deadline).
- Covers: Up to $5,000/year ($2,500/semester) for full-time enrollment in 2025-26.
- Renewal: FAFSA by October 1 each year, maintain SAI/income thresholds, satisfactory academic progress.
- Duration: 10 semesters or 15 quarters (5 years).
- Key details: The October 1 FAFSA deadline is critical  missing it forfeits OCOG for the entire year. Available at both public and private nonprofit Ohio institutions.

**Buckeye Bridge Initiative (Ohio State + Columbus State)**
- Requirements: Graduate from Columbus State Community College with an associate degree. Family AGI $100,000 or less. Must file FAFSA by February 1. Must transfer to Ohio State within 3 semesters of graduating Columbus State.
- Covers: Full waiver of Ohio State tuition and mandatory fees. Does not cover room, board, or books.
- Duration: Up to 8 semesters at Ohio State.
- Key details: Launches Spring 2026. Partnership between two specific institutions only, not statewide. The $100,000 threshold is generous.

**Scaffold note:** For Ohio families, the plan MUST emphasize the October 1 FAFSA deadline for OCOG  one of the earliest in the country. For Columbus-area families under $100K, Buckeye Bridge (Columbus State to Ohio State, tuition-free) should be highlighted as a potentially transformative option. Note it's brand new (2026) and the 3-semester transfer window is tight.

### UTAH -- Utah Promise, Utah Opportunity Scholarship

**Utah Promise Scholarship (Institutional Programs)**
- Requirements: Vary by USHE campus. At University of Utah (launching Fall 2026): first-time first-year, graduated HS 2026+, family income and assets each under $100,000, apply by December 1, FAFSA by February 1.
- Covers: Full tuition and mandatory fees (last-dollar). Does not typically cover room, board, or books.
- Renewal: At U of U: 3.5 cumulative GPA, full-time enrollment. Other campuses: satisfactory academic progress, FAFSA annually, typically 24 credits/year.
- Duration: Up to 4 years.
- Key details: Not a single statewide program  each USHE campus has its own version with different thresholds. The U of U's 3.5 GPA renewal requirement is notably high and could cause students to lose the scholarship.

**Utah Opportunity Scholarship (Statewide Merit, replaced Regents' Scholarship)**
- Requirements: Utah HS graduate, 3.3+ GPA, at least 1 AP/IB/CE course in each of Math, Science, and Language Arts (3 total). Must complete FAFSA.
- Covers: Up to $1,000/semester for up to 4 semesters. Maximum $4,000 total.
- Duration: 2 years (4 semesters).
- Key details: Modest but meaningful given Utah's low public tuition. Students must plan HS course selections to include qualifying AP/IB/CE courses. Application for Class of 2026: January 5 to March 20.

**Scaffold note:** For need-based Utah families, identify which USHE campus's promise program has the best terms. Flag U of U's 3.5 GPA renewal risk. The Opportunity Scholarship is a helpful supplement but at $4,000 total, not a primary funding source. Utah's in-state tuition is already quite low, making any state aid more impactful percentage-wise.

### WISCONSIN -- Wisconsin Grant

**Wisconsin Grant**
- Requirements: Wisconsin resident, undergraduate, must complete FAFSA, demonstrate financial need. Four sub-programs: WG-UW (UW System), WG-WTC (Technical Colleges), WG-TR (Tribal), WG-PNP (Private Nonprofits).
- Covers: $875 to $3,500/year for full-time enrollment in 2025-26. Prorated for part-time: 9-11 credits = 75%, 6-8 credits = 50%, 1-5 credits = 25%. WG-PNP: approximately $250 to $3,150.
- Renewal: Annual FAFSA, continued financial need, satisfactory academic progress.
- Duration: Maximum 10 semesters across all four sub-programs.
- Key details: Four separate programs unified under one name, each with its own funding pool and award formulas set annually by HEAB. Wisconsin has no statewide merit scholarship program.

**Scaffold note:** For Wisconsin families, note the Wisconsin Grant as a need-based supplement to Pell ($875-$3,500). Flag that Wisconsin offers no statewide merit scholarship, so institutional merit awards from individual campuses (UW-Madison, Marquette, etc.) are where the larger scholarship dollars come from. The 10-semester cap means students who take longer than 5 years lose state grant eligibility.

## NOTES FOR THE SCAFFOLD PROMPT

These rules apply across all plans, regardless of the family's state.

1. **Always identify the family's state-specific programs by name.** The plan should never say "look into your state's programs." It should name the program, state the requirements, and tell the family whether their kid is on track to qualify.

2. **Frame merit thresholds as concrete goals.** "Protect the 3.7 GPA" (Georgia). "Get to 1200 SAT" (Georgia). "Hit ACT 27 for TOPS Honors" (Louisiana). These are actionable targets that belong in the developmental roadmap.

3. **Flag early-action deadlines explicitly.** Indiana 21st Century Scholars (must enroll by 8th grade), Oklahoma's Promise (apply by 8th-12th grade, earlier is better), and FAFSA deadlines that vary by state (Ohio: October 1, Illinois MAP: as early as possible, Iowa Future Ready: December 31). If the student is young enough to act on these, they belong in the "do this now" section of the plan.

4. **Model state aid as a layer, not a standalone number.** Show families the full stack: Pell + state grant + institutional merit + federal loans = net cost. Never present a state program in isolation. A $4,500 Tennessee HOPE on top of a $7,395 Pell on top of a $10,000 institutional merit award tells a very different story than HOPE alone.

5. **Distinguish between guaranteed and competitive programs.** Georgia HOPE/Zell Miller, Florida Bright Futures, and Kentucky KEES are entitlement programs: meet the criteria, get the money. Montana MUS Honor (200 awards), Alabama ASAP (first-come-first-served), and Colorado Student Grant (limited pool) are competitive or rationed. The plan should be clear about which category each program falls into so families know what they can count on vs. what they should hope for.

6. **Flag residency requirements and post-graduation obligations.** New York Excelsior converts to a loan if you leave NY. Pennsylvania Grow PA converts to a loan if you leave PA. Kansas Promise requires 2 years of living/working in Kansas. Rhode Island Promise requires living/working in RI. These are real financial commitments that constrain a student's post-graduation options. The plan should present them as trade-offs, not free money.

7. **Handle public vs. private school strategy per state.** In Iowa, the Tuition Grant + institutional match can make private colleges cheaper than out-of-state publics. In Georgia, Zell Miller makes public colleges nearly free but provides nothing at private schools. In California, Cal Grant A covers UC tuition but the Middle Class Scholarship extends to families earning up to $234K. The plan should know which states reward staying public, which reward going private, and where the math is close enough to warrant modeling both.

8. **Check regional exchange eligibility.** If the family is in a WUE state (western), MSEP state (midwest), SREB state (southern), or New England RSP state, flag relevant exchange programs when suggesting out-of-state public schools. A Montana student considering University of Oregon should know about WUE (150% of in-state). A Kentucky student interested in a program not offered in-state should know about the Academic Common Market.

9. **Account for DC's unique situation.** DC has no state university. DC TAG ($15K/year at public universities nationwide) fundamentally changes the math for DC families. Any plan for a DC family should model target public universities with DC TAG applied. Do not assume DC students pay full out-of-state tuition.

10. **Never fabricate state program names or details.** If you are unsure whether a state has a specific program, do not invent one. It is better to say "check with [state]'s higher education agency for current state aid programs" than to cite a program that doesn't exist or has been discontinued. Families will try to find these programs and will lose trust if the names are wrong.

11. **Note when programs are new or recently changed.** Louisiana TOPS Excellence (added 2025), Pennsylvania Grow PA (started 2025-26), Ohio Buckeye Bridge (launching 2026), North Dakota Scholarship (replaced Academic/CTE for Class of 2025+). New programs may have limited public awareness, so families may not have heard of them. Flag newness as a reason to verify details directly with the school or state agency.

12. **Prioritize the highest-impact programs for each family.** Not every state program is worth mentioning. For a Georgia family earning $60K, Zell Miller is the entire financial strategy and deserves a full paragraph. The FAFSA state deadline deserves a sentence. A $500 supplemental grant can be mentioned in passing or skipped. Match the depth of coverage to the financial impact for that specific family.

13. **Check gap year compatibility.** Multiple state programs require enrollment immediately after high school graduation: Missouri Bright Flight, Arizona Promise, Tennessee Promise, Florida Bright Futures (August 31 deadline). If a family is considering a gap year, explicitly check whether each identified state program allows deferred enrollment and for how long. Flag any programs that would be forfeited.

14. **Handle military families carefully.** Military families move frequently. Check whether the student's state of legal residence or the state where they attend high school determines eligibility. Many states have special military dependent provisions (e.g., Florida Bright Futures, Georgia HOPE).

15. **Address divorced/separated parent situations.** When parents live in different states, state aid eligibility typically follows the custodial parent's state (matching FAFSA). Note that the non-custodial parent's state programs are generally NOT available to the student.

16. **Flag last-dollar program dynamics.** When a state program is last-dollar (Minnesota North Star Promise, Arizona Promise, New Hampshire Granite Guarantee, etc.), winning additional outside scholarships does NOT reduce the family's out-of-pocket cost -- it reduces the state award instead. Explain this clearly so families understand that "more scholarships" does not always mean "less cost" with last-dollar programs.

17. **Warn about dual enrollment credit-hour caps.** If the student has or plans to earn dual enrollment credits, check whether those credits count against the state scholarship's credit-hour cap. Georgia HOPE/Zell Miller's 127-hour cap is the most common example -- dual enrollment hours earned in high school count against it, potentially shortening the scholarship duration in college.
